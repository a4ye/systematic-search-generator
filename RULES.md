You only have access to info you would have access to during a real sysematic review.
That is, you can only get access to a small number of random seed papers (around 3), and you cannot cherry pick them
since you can't do that in the real world.
You will also have access to the PROSPERO record, which includes the review title, abstract, and inclusion criteria, as
well as the search strategy if it is provided. You can use this information to generate your search query and identify
relevant studies. However, you will not have access to the full text of the included studies or any additional
information beyond what is provided in the PROSPERO record and the seed papers.
Note that the benchmarks compares with the real list of included studies which you wouldn't have in the real world, so
you cannot use the benchmark as feedback to improve your query during a run. Though you can use it to improve the
code/process for future runs.

DO NOT hardcode terms based on the benchmark results in an attempt to maximize the benchmark scores. This will overfit
and will not be generalizable to new reviews. Instead, focus on developing a robust query generation process that can
perform well across a variety of review topics and types, using only the information that would be realistically
available during a systematic review.
